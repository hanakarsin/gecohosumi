CS Kagwe tells lovers to ask for blood donation certificates on Valentine's Day instead of flowers

- CS Kagwe said the best proof of love was a blood donation certificate instead of flowers

- The CS said if lovers have to choose whether they want flowers or the certificates, they should choose the latter

- Kagwe said as blood is freely given to every Kenyan by God, they should also donate it freely

Health Cabinet Secretary Mutahi Kagwe has asked lovers to ask for blood donation certificates on Valentine's Day instead of flowers.

While speaking about the importance of blood donation in the country, Kagwe said the best proof of love was a blood donation certificate instead of a bouquet of flowers.

Speaking after officiating Valentine's day blood donation at Kenya National Blood Transfusion Centre, the CS said if lovers have to choose whether they want flowers or the certificates, they should choose the latter in order to replenish Kenya's blood bank.

"If there is a fantastic gift to give during Valentine's Day it will be blood. If someone wants to give you flowers, also ask them to donate blood," he said.

Kagwe said as blood is freely given to every Kenyan by God, they should also donate it freely to people who may need it.

"I am informed by doctors that when you give blood, your health actually improves. I, therefore, urge Kenyans to donate blood," he said.

A few months ago, the Ministry of Health appealed to Kenyans to volunteer in donating blood to the National Blood Transfusion Service in a bid to save the country from impending acute shortage.

The ministry noted the situation of the country's blood bank was challenging and urgent interventions were needed to restock it.

The country has been grappling with the challenge of blood shortages with reports indicating some rogue officials have been colluding with medics and unscrupulous business people to sell the blood in other countries.

In March 2020, Kagwe acknowledged that there are criminal elements, sometimes within the Health ministry, colluding with outsiders to escalate the shortage problem.

According to reports, donated blood was mostly being sold in Somalia by an enterprise of cartels from the National Blood Transfusion Service and officials from the ministry.

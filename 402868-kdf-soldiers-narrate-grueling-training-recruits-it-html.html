KDF soldiers narrate grueling training as recruits: &quot;It was hell&quot;

- A YouTube video titled 'A KDF Soldier' takes a trip through the rigorous training that changes recruits from civilians to military men and women

- According to Colonel Meshack Kishoyan, the skills imparted are both offensive and defensive to drill the trainees well for the task ahead

- Individuals on medication, those who have had substance abuse issues, and those with terminal illnesses cannot be accepted as recruits because the rigorous exercises would aggravate the conditions

Year in, year out, thousands of youths from all walks of life descend on recruitment centers to try their luck at gaining entry into the Kenyan military.

Some are rejected for one reason or the other, others are recruited but fall by the wayside, while the resolute ones hold on until the culmination of the training at a pass out parade religiously graced by the commander in chief of the armed forces, the country's sitting president.

In a YouTube video titled 'A KDF Soldier,' the one time-recruits took a trip down memory lane to the rigorous training that changed them from civilians to military men and women.

According to Chief Instructor Colonel Meshack Kishoyan, the skills imparted in the trainees are both offensive and defensive to ensure that they protect themselves from the enemy.

"This training is very difficult, so it calls for courage, and we must develop this over a long period of time," he explained.

He adds that recruits who join the training without proper physical, mental and moral preparation break down along the way.

"I remember it very well. It was hell. I didn't really like the training the way it began, but I had to accept it," narrated Elena Opole.

"When we got there, a whistle blew. I do not want to say what happened that day, but it remains an experience I will always share with my children," remembered Dickson Onchera.

Genevieve Ojwang underpins that although the training was difficult, her fighting spirit kept her grounded in it.

Given the task involves being on the frontline in protecting the country against potential enemies, physical fitness is a basic requirement for those wishing to be part of the military.

Brigadier Maroa Gati explains that the training involves lessons in career development, marksmanship, drill and duty, field craft and physical fitness.

"Prospective recruits and trainees are urged to declare if they have got any underlying medical conditions and this is for their wellbeing and safety," explained Col Bernard Edalia.

He points out that individuals on medication, those who have had substance abuse issues, and those with terminal illnesses cannot be accepted as recruits because the rigorous physical exercises would aggravate the conditions.
